How the IRS Operates

The Internal Revenue Service is the federal agency responsible for carrying out and enforcing the nation’s tax laws. It handles the flow of tax returns each year, processes payments, and issues refunds when people have paid more than they owe. Beyond that, the IRS is also responsible for reviewing returns and addressing situations where something does not seem right. While many people associate the agency with audits or penalties, much of its work is routine and focused on keeping the overall tax system running smoothly.

To make sure tax information is accurate, the IRS collects records from employers, banks, and other financial institutions and compares them to what taxpayers report on their returns. When the numbers do not match, the return may be flagged for further review. In many cases this is done through automated systems designed to catch common mistakes, but IRS employees also step in for more complicated cases. These reviews are meant to correct errors and resolve issues, even though they can feel stressfull for taxpayers involved.

The IRS also plays an important role in helping people understand how to follow tax laws. It updates tax forms, releases instructions, and publishes guidance when rules change or need clarification. To support taxpayers directly, the agency offers online tools to check refund status, access records, and find forms when avaliable. For those who need more personal help, local assistance centers and partner programs provide in person support in many communities, making the process a little less confusing for those who need extra guidance.

Main functions

  • Processing tax returns and issuing refunds in a secure, scheduled workflow.
  • Collection of unpaid taxes and managing taxpayer accounts, including installment agreements and offers in compromise.
  • Audits and examinations to verify accuracy and prevent fraud or evasion.
  • Providing taxpayer assistance and education through online resources, phone lines, and community partners like VITA.

Communication

The IRS primarily uses mail for official notices. It will not threaten immediate arrest or demand payment by unusual methods. Be cautious of phone, text, or email scams that impersonate the IRS.

Accept legitimate IRS correspondence by checking return addresses, using IRS online tools to verify notices, and contacting the IRS directly via published phone numbers if you are unsure.
