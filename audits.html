Understanding Audits & Your Rights

An audit is an IRS review to verify accuracy. Types include correspondence, office, and field audits.

How Audits Work

Audits can be random or triggered by discrepancies. The IRS will request documentation. Respond promptly and keep records organized.

Audits can be random or triggered by discrepancies between reported income and third‑party information (W-2s, 1099s), unusually large deductions, or suspicious patterns. The IRS may request documentation to substantiate items on the return.

When selected, you'll receive a notice explaining the type of audit and what documentation is required. Common audit types include correspondence audits (by mail), office audits (in-person review at an IRS office), and field audits (on-site). Respond on time and with complete records to reduce escalation.

Keep copies of receipts, canceled checks, bank statements, and clear explanations for income or expenses. If you don't agree, you have the right to appeal and present additional evidence.

Your Rights

Taxpayer Bill of Rights guarantees the right to representation, privacy, and appeal. You may have a qualified representative speak on your behalf.

Taxpayer Bill of Rights guarantees the right to representation, privacy, and appeal. You may have a qualified representative speak on your behalf.

The rights include the right to be informed about the tax laws, the right to quality service from IRS staff, and the right to a fair and just tax system. If audited, you can request an explanation, meet with the examiner, and receive a written decision.

If you can't resolve issues through the IRS, the Taxpayer Advocate Service is an independent organization that helps taxpayers facing financial hardship or systemic problems with IRS processes.

Avoiding Problems

  • Keep organized records for at least 3 years.
  • Keep supporting documentation for major deductions and credits for up to seven years when required (for certain claims and audits).
  • Double-check math and report all income.
  • Use tax software or professional review to reduce common errors.
  • Respond to IRS notices by the date provided and keep copies of all correspondence.

Quick tips

  • Keep receipts and bank statements.
  • Seek help from LITC if low-income and facing disputes.
