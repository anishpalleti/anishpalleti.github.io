Resources / Things to Avoid

Fraudulent Tax Preparers

Beware of preparers who promise huge refunds, suggest falsifying information, or demand payment in cash only. Use credentialed preparers: CPAs, enrolled agents (EAs), or IRS-certified VITA volunteers.

Fraudulent preparers may pressure you to sign incomplete forms, include false deductions, or list dependents who do not qualify. These actions can lead to penalties, interest, and possible legal consequences for the taxpayer.

Before choosing a preparer, verify credentials, ask about experience with low-income taxpayers, and request a sample or references. Always get a copy of the completed return and review it carefully before signing.

How to protect yourself

  • Check preparer credentials and PTIN registration.
  • Verify preparer qualifications and ask for references. Confirm they file returns electronically with their own EFIN.
  • Never sign a blank return.
  • Keep copies of your tax return and supporting documents.
  • Store sensitive documents securely and shred financial records you no longer need.
  • Verify identity of anyone claiming to be from the IRS. Official contact comes by mail.
  • Use the IRS website or published phone numbers to confirm any request. Do not click suspicious links or provide personal information over unsolicited calls.
  • Compare the refund amount and entries on the signed copy with what you expect. Report discrepancies immediately.
  • If something seems off, seek a second opinion from another preparer or a VITA/LITC site before paying or submitting the return.

After a scam

If you suspect fraud, contact the IRS Identity Protection Specialized Unit and follow instructions to file Form 14039 if identity theft is suspected.

Report suspicious preparers using Form 14157 and contact the Taxpayer Advocate Service if you face delays or hardship. Keep all records of communication and preserve copies of suspicious messages for authorities.
