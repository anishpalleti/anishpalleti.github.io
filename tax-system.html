How the U.S. Tax System Works

The U.S. tax system funds public services and is administered by federal, state, and local governments. Federal income tax is collected by the IRS on behalf of the federal government, while states and localities may collect additional taxes for their purposes.

Taxes support services like Social Security, Medicare, public safety, transportation, and education. Different taxes (income, payroll, sales) operate together to fund government functions.

Understanding the tax system helps you file correctly, claim benefits, and avoid common mistakes that can lead to penalties or delays.

Who pays federal income tax?

Most individuals and businesses that earn income above certain thresholds are required to file and pay federal income taxes.

Filing thresholds depend on filing status, age, and type of income. Even if you're below the threshold, filing may be beneficial to claim refundable credits or recover withheld taxes.

Businesses, self-employed individuals, and investors have additional filing requirements, including estimated tax payments and informational returns (1099s).

Progressive tax rates

Individual income tax uses tax brackets where higher portions of income are taxed at higher rates. This is called a progressive tax structure.

Only the income within each bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate, not all of a taxpayer's income. Taxable income is calculated after adjustments, deductions, and credits.

Understanding bracket mechanics helps with planning and estimating tax liability, especially if you expect income changes during the year.

Key concepts

  • Taxable income: income after adjustments, deductions, and exemptions.
  • Adjusted gross income (AGI): a starting point used for many calculations and eligibility tests.
  • Withholding: employers may withhold taxes from paychecks to cover federal income tax liability.
  • Estimated taxes: paid by self-employed individuals on a quarterly basis to cover expected tax liability.
  • Tax credits: dollar-for-dollar reductions of tax liability (some refundable, some not).
  • Tax deductions: reduce taxable income and may lower the tax owed.
