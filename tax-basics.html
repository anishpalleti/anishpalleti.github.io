Understanding Your Taxes

How the IRS Works

The IRS administers federal tax laws, processes returns, issues refunds, and enforces compliance.

It collects information from employers, financial institutions, and taxpayers to match reported income and credits, and it uses automated checks plus manual review to identify possible discrepancies.

The agency also issues guidance, publishes forms and instructions, and provides taxpayer assistance through phone lines, online tools, and community programs.

1. Earn

Income is earned (wages, tips, self-employment, interest, etc.).

2. Report

You report income and credits on a tax return, plus W-2/1099 forms filed by employers and banks.

3. Process

IRS verifies and matches forms, calculates tax owed or refund, and may request more information.

4. Refund / Notice

If overpaid, you get a refund. If there’s an issue or balance due, the IRS sends a notice.

Tip: Keep records (W-2s/1099s, receipts, and notices). Respond promptly if you receive an IRS letter.

Income Tax Returns Explained

Tax returns are forms submitted annually (Form 1040 for most individuals) that report income, credits, deductions, and determine tax owed or refund.

Most returns include schedules and attachments for specific types of income (self-employment, rental, investment) and adjustments to income such as educator expenses or student loan interest.

Filing accurately helps prevent delays or audits. Report all income and provide supporting documentation when requested.

Common parts of a tax return

  • Personal information and filing status
  • Income (wages, self-employment, interest, dividends)
  • Deductions and credits
  • Tax computation and payments

What Are Income Tax Returns?

An income tax return reports your income, deductions, credits, and tax liability to the IRS. Individuals typically file Form 1040.

What to report

  • Wages, salary, and tips (Form W-2).
  • Self-employment income (Schedule C).
  • Interest, dividends, and other income.

Filing and deadlines

Most individuals file by the annual deadline (usually April). Extensions may be available for filing, but taxes owed are still due on time.

Step-by-Step Filing Guide

  1. Collect income forms (W-2, 1099)
  2. Gather receipts and documents for deductions
  3. Organize proof for credits (childcare, education, earned income)
  4. Determine eligibility for free programs (VITA/LITC) and schedule an appointment if eligible
  5. File electronically or using an approved preparer and verify the preparer's credentials
  6. Track refund online, keep your confirmation, and respond promptly to any IRS notices

Filing Requirements

Filing requirements depend on income, filing status, age, and other factors. Even if you are below thresholds, filing can help you claim refunds.

First-Time Filer Tips

  • Double-check all Social Security numbers.
  • Verify names and dates of birth match Social Security records to avoid mismatches.
  • Keep copies of returns and supporting documents for at least three years.
  • Organize a simple folder with W-2s, 1099s, and receipts so you can respond quickly to requests.
  • Avoid preparers who demand large upfront fees or offer unrealistic refunds.
  • Ask for the preparer's PTIN and a printed copy of the return before you pay.
